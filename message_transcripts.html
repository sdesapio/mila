Alice's message (Monday, May 25, 9:29pm)
"As head coach, I only allow secondary coaches do touch ups to choreography..."

From the very beginning, Mila made clear to Alice that the family was new to the figure skating world and didn't know how things worked — and that they were heavily relying on Alice's guidance. No one ever explained the concept of a head coach, what the role entailed, what authority it carried, or what agreeing to it would mean in practice.

Factual Dispute — "Industry Standard" Claim
Alice's claim
"Moving forward, please have a conversation with me first before reaching out or speaking with other coaches first. This is the standard amongst skating coaches."

This claim is inaccurate. The U.S. Figure Skating Coaching Standards of Professionalism contain no rule requiring an athlete or parent to obtain permission from their coach before speaking with or consulting another coach. Alice presented a personal preference as if it were an industry-wide obligation — it is not.

In fact, the actual USFS standard runs in the opposite direction: it explicitly protects the athlete's right to consult other coaches and places the professional obligation on coaches, not families. See the USFS Research section below for the full findings.

It is also worth noting that prior to scheduling the Bryan trial lesson, every reasonable courtesy was extended: Alice was informed in advance, the purpose was explained clearly, her perspective was invited, the lesson was framed as a one-time trial, and Bryan himself confirmed Alice's approval before proceeding. This conduct exceeded even the informal etiquette standards described in non-USFS sources.

USFS Coaching Standards — Research Findings

What USFS Actually Says

Source: U.S. Figure Skating Coaching Standards of Professionalism

No rule was found requiring an athlete or parent to obtain permission from their coach before speaking with, consulting, or taking a lesson from another coach. The official USFS standard states:

"Coaches will recognize the Participant's right to change coaching arrangements or consult with other coaches/advisors."

This obligation is placed on the coach, not the family. It explicitly protects the athlete's right to consult other coaches.

Third-Party Sources — Etiquette, Not Rules

Two non-USFS sources describe a professional etiquette expectation that coaches communicate with one another and respect existing relationships. These sources are skatereddeer.com (Red Deer Skating Club Code of Ethics) and Law Insider (Coach Ethical Conduct). Importantly, this etiquette obligation applies to coaches — not to athletes or families — and neither source is a USFS document.

Rule vs. Etiquette

Statement Status Source
Athlete must get permission from coach before talking to another coach NOT FOUND No USFS rule exists
Athlete may consult other coaches EXPLICITLY PROTECTED USFS Coaching Standards of Professionalism
Coaches should communicate professionally and respect existing relationships ETIQUETTE ONLY Non-USFS sources (applies to coaches, not families)

Bottom Line

Alice asserted a rule that does not exist in USFS policy. The actual USFS standard runs in the opposite direction — it explicitly protects the athlete's right to consult other coaches and places the professional obligation on coaches, not families. The demand for written pre-approval before any coach contact has no basis in USFS policy.
